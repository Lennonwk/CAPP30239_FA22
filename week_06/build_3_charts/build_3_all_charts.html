People Killed By US Police in 2015

Deceased Age and Gender Demographics

I chose to construct a histogram style stacked bar chart to depict the age distribution of the deceased. Due to the nature of the data, I chose not to use a histogram because so few of the deceased people were younger than 15 or older than 75. An interesting detail highlighted by this chart is the number of males and females under the age of 20 who were killed is almost equal which is different from all other age groups. This is an area for deeper investigation.

Deceased Race and Potential to Harm Others

The Race ring chart displays the racial distribution of people killed by police. I chose a ring chart because this layout allows the reader to quickly gain a sense of the percentages of the the three races most killed by police. A reader would be able to draw more conclusions if this data was adjusted for the percentage of each race in the US population.

The Armed ring chart depicts whether the deceased individual was carrying a weapon at the time of their death. The fact that nearly half of the individuals were carrying a gun may indicate that the police officer was acting in self-defense. However, it's difficult to draw conclusions due to the high number of deceased with unreported weapons statuses.

Top 3 States For Police Involved Deaths by Month

California, Texas, and Florida account for more than 35% of all police killings in 2015. I chose to create a multi-line chart to determine if there were seasonal trends across all the states or if national events were influencing crime increases or decreases. The depiction indicates no police killing correlation between the states. While these states had the most police killings, they are also the 3 most populous states in the country. It would be interesting to compare police killings per capita for each state.

These charts are derived from data obtained from the Washington Post and curated by Professor Tiffany France for a UChicago MSCAPP Data Visualization assignment.
