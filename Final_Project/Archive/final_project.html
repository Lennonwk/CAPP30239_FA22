Who Will Pay for The Aging World?

Additional Years Means Additional Cost

Significant advances in medicine and food production have contributed to more than doubling the world average life expectancy at birth since 1900. However, an inconvenient consequence of rising life expectancy combined with a falling fertility rate is the financial burden of supporting senior citizens. Governments must acknowledge current social program cost trajectories and act now to manage citizens’ expectations of unsustainable entitlement benefits.

Pension payments and government funded medical cost make senior citizens the most expensive individual demographic for national governments. Few countries have successfully stabilized pension spending relative to output per working-age person from since 1990. Elected officials jeopardize reelection if they attempt to reduce social security types of assistance. Senior citizens tend have a disproportionate level of political influence due to higher voter participation and campaign contributions when compared to younger voters. As the average life expectancy has increased, the average number of pension payments received over an individual’s lifetime has increased as well. Additionally, researchers have estimated that 48.1% of an American’s lifetime healthcare cost occur after the age of 64. During this period of life, Americans are more likely to receive government funded healthcare vice an individually or employer funded insurance policy.

Doing More with Less

The number of working age relative to retired people developed and developing countries has significantly declined over the last 30 years and this United Nations Population Division expects this trend to continue. The International Monetary Fund assesses that this demographic shift will result in the slowing of gross domestic product (GDP) growth . “Many empirical studies have found that GDP growth slows roughly one to one with declines in labor force and population growth – a disquieting prospect for both the United States and Europe.” The decreased worker to retiree ratio will result in fewer income tax revenue sources per retiree and the reduction in GDP growth will negatively impact corporate tax revenue. All of these factors will exacerbate the rising pension costs as a percentage of GDP burdens on governments.

Help Is NOT On the Way

Countries should not expect the increase in 65+ year-olds to be balanced by an increase in young people. A majority of the working age population (20-64) and all of the retiree age population (65+) have already been born and the math does not look promising. Fertility rates during the last 50 years have decreased for every country with Organization for Economic and Development Cooperation (OECD) recorded data . Researchers correlate the fertility rate decline to women gaining increased access to education, employment opportunities, and contraception. This increased access is not expected to reverse course at the world level.

What Can Be Done?

National governments must act now to set realistic expectations for future retirees. At the country level, governments that currently have some of the lowest worker to retiree ratios can liberalize immigration policies. However, this is a short-term acute solution that does not address the long-term global issue. The difficult decisions may involve raising the eligibility age of pension and social security entitlements or reducing benefits. These reductions in future spending can be paired with incentivizing individual retirement savings accounts or defined contribution plans for workers to build their own financial independence. These policy changes will be unpopular with workers, but bad news does not get better with time. Necessary policy changes will only grow more drastic as policy makers delay action. National governments should use Japan as a leading indicator of what’s to come. “While Japan’s total population is projected to decrease by 11 million, the population age 65 and over is projected to increase by 8 million between 2006 and 2030.” Japan has already reached a 2.5 worker to retiree ratio that projected for America in 2050.
